Tutorial 7: Market Basket Analysis

Brief Description: Market Basket Analysis is one of the most common and useful types of data analysis for marketing and retailing.  The purpose of market basket analysis is to determine what products customers purchase together.  It takes its name from the idea of customers throwing all their purchases into a shopping cart (a "market basket") during grocery shopping.  Knowing what products people purchase as a group can be very helpful to a retailer or to any other company.  A store could use this information to place products frequently sold together into the same area, while a catalog or World Wide Web merchant could use it to determine the layout of their catalog and order form.  Direct marketers could use the basket analysis results to determine what new products to offer their prior customers.

 

In some cases, the fact that items sell together is obvious – every fast-food restaurant asks their customers "Would you like fries with that?" whenever they go through a drive-through window.  However, sometimes the fact that certain items would sell well together is far from obvious.  A well-known example is that a supermarket performing a basket analysis discovered that diapers and beer sell well together on Thursdays.  Though the result does make sense – young couples stocking up on supplies for themselves and for their children before the weekend starts – it’s not the sort of thing that someone would normally think of right away.  The strength of market basket analysis is that by using computer data mining tools, it’s not necessary for a person to think of what products consumers would logically buy together – instead, the customers’ sales data is allowed to speak for itself.  This is a good example of data-driven marketing.

 

Once it is known that customers who buy one product are likely to buy another, it is possible for the company to market the products together, or to make the purchasers of one product the target prospects for another.  If customers who purchase diapers are already likely to purchase beer, they’ll be even more likely to if there happens to be a beer display just outside the diaper aisle.  Likewise, if it’s known that customers who buy a sweater and casual pants from a certain mail-order catalog have a propensity toward buying a jacket from the same catalog, sales of jackets can be increased by having the telephone representatives describe and offer the jacket to anyone who calls in to order the sweater and pants.  Still better, the catalogue company can provide an additional 5% discount on a package containing the sweater, pants, and jacket simultaneously and promote well the complete package.  The dollar amount of sales is guaranteed to go up.  By targeting customers who are already known to be likely buyers, the effectiveness of marketing is significantly increased – regardless of if the marketing takes the form of in-store displays, catalog layout design, or direct offers to customers.  This is the purpose of market basket analysis – to improve the effectiveness of marketing and sales tactics using customer data already available to the company.

 

In this tutorial, we will examine a manufacturer of electric appliances and components.  Our example company has over 250 products, each identified by a 3-character code.  The goal of this exploration is to find out which of these products sell well together, so that when a customer or distributor purchases some products, the manufacturer’s sales representatives can offer related products with an increased chance of making a sale.

Step 2a:  Opening the Data Set and Understanding its Contents

Now, we’ll examine the World dataset.

1.      Double click on the World data set in the tree view.  The dataset will be displayed.

About the World Data Set: In this dataset, each row represents a transaction, and each column represents a product code.  Each cell, then, contains a yes/no value representing if that product was included in that transaction.  Because of this structure, the great majority of the cells will be "no" values – each transaction usually only includes a dozen or so products out of the 255.  This is not a problem for the Basket Analysis algorithm, as this is the preferred structure of a transaction file for basket analysis.

 

In this case, we are looking for products that sell well together in a single transaction (at the same time) – when a distributor calls or sends a purchase order, we want to recommend products that we can sell immediately.  If the analysis were being done for a supermarket, we would have a similar goal – to place items that sell well together at the same time together in the store, so that customers will be more likely to engage in impulse buying.

 

In some cases, however (such as for a music or book club, or any kind of relationship-marketing endeavor), we are less interested in what products sell to a given customer at the same time than in what products a customer with a given historical buying record is likely to want to buy.  In that situation, Basket Analysis could still be used – however, the rows would represent customers, rather than transactions.  This might result in a higher number of "yes" values, relative to "no"s, in the table, but the algorithm will work just as well.

 

Step 2b:  Beginning the Exploration

We will begin by running a preliminary basket analysis on our World dataset.

1.      Right-click on the World data set icon, and choose Explore | Basket Analysis from the pop-up menu.

The Basket Analysis dialog is displayed:

About the Basket Analysis Dialog:  Here, we can choose which fields to include, as well as set five options: Binarize numeric values, Minimum support (or Minimum association volume for numerical data), Minimum improvement, Minimum confidence, and Process Name.

 

Binarize numeric values:  If your data reflects only the fact whether or not a certain product has been purchased in a given transaction, then standard Market Basket Analysis allows you to find what other products might be purchased in the same transaction.  Standard market basket analysis would be useful, for example, for an e-commerce web site (like Amazon.com), since it tells you which other book titles you should suggest to a customer who purchased this particular book.  However, you also might possess the information on the purchase volume (monetary or in units bought) for each individual product.  If you attempt using the traditional basket analysis in this case, all this additional information is simply lost.  Yet often, this information might provide an additional insight into the situation. If you want to exploit this additional information – you need some more advanced, non-traditional analysis techniques in this case.  PolyAnalyst provides you with two modifications of a unique Market Basket Analysis algorithm: one that can make use of numeric sale volumes available for different products, another that converts all available sale volumes to "yes/no" type and solves a regular basket analysis task.

 

The Binarize Numeric Values option allows the Basket Analysis algorithm to be used to convert numeric data to "yes/no" data types.  If this box is checked, 0 values are treated as "no", and all other values are treated as "yes".  Thus, if your transactional data were in the form of sales volumes, quantities, etc., it would still be possible to run the traditional Basket Analysis without having to convert the data to yes/no values manually.

2.      Keep the Binarize Numberic Value option checked since we are not dealing with any volume or price attributes.

Minimum support/ Minimum association volume:  This control is used to fine-tune the basket analysis.  For yes/no data this control comes in the form of Minimum support level in percent.  It sets the minimum part of transactions that should contain a basket of products in order to consider this basket as a distinct stable product group.  By default this value is equal to 10%. If this number is set high, only products whose co-occurrence is in a very large number of transactions will be considered.  This will result in a small number of product clusters being found, and each cluster containing only a few products (often 2).  A high value is desirable when looking for 1-to-1 rules ("If Product A sold in this transaction, Product B will probably sell also.") A lower minimum association volume will force those products which occur together less frequently, to be considered as market baskets (product clusters).  This results in many, larger clusters.  This can be desirable, for example, if you are organizing a store or catalog and simply wish to know which products to place in the same area of the store.

 

For numeric data, Minimum association volume should be set either in the corresponding currency units or in units of products purchased.  The default value is 0 in this case – and should be changed by the user in order to achieve better results.

 

In the considered example, we are going to first leave Minimum support default value of 10% of all the transactions.  Thus, only products that occur together in 10% or more of all transactions will be included in some basket in this analysis.

 

Minimum improvement:  Improvement indicates how much better the Confidence of the found directed association rule is than that obtained by random guessing. Improvement roughly corresponds to how much more money a retailer might generate by exploiting the association rule found by PolyAnalyst Market Basket Analysis.  The default value of Minimum improvement is set to 2.

 

Minimum confidence:  Confidence is a probability, that if a customer purchases a considered group of items, he is going to purchase the other considered item.  The higher is the confidence for a rule, the more value this rule has for real world applications.  Yet, if the Minimum confidence is set too high, we might find no association rules providing such confidence.  The user should experimentally determine an optimal value for Minimum confidence. The default value is 65%.

 

Process Name:  The process name is, like in all exploration engines, used to determine the name of any reports and datasets created by the Basket Analysis exploration.  By default, the name will be BA_DatasetName, in this case BA_World.  This is fine if you intend to run only one basket analysis on a given dataset, but in this case we will be running three, each with a different minimum association volume.  Thus, we need to name the project.

3.      Name the Basket Analysis “BA_10_2_65” in the process name text box.

4.      We will run Minimum support at 10% (or about 117 transactions) in this exploration.  Enter 10 into the Minimum support text box and click OK

 

Step 3: Analyzing the Results

Now we will analyze the basket analysis report.

Unlike most basket analysis algorithms available, PolyAnalyst’s algorithm is extremely fast and should be done in less than a minute. The first part of the report appears as follows:

 

About the Report:  As you can see, five different groups of products were found that have support 10% or greater (the illustration above is only a partial listing due to the length of the report).  The first group contains six products: 06K, 02C, 02D, 01D, 01C and 01B.  The second group contains three products: 03X, 02B, and 01K. In addition, each group has the corresponding p-value listed – the lower this value is, the less statistically likely it is that the group appeared by random chance.  Often, the p-values are so small that one needs to show the ten-base logarithm in order to display the p-value.  The product association volume (how many times the group of products occurred together in a single transaction) is also listed.

 

In addition to being listed in the report, each of these product clusters is created as a new dataset – entitled BA_10_2_65_1, BA_10_2_65_2, and so on.  This is one reason it is important to name the process when running Basket Analysis – otherwise, it can become confusing to know which datasets came from which basket analysis.  In this case, these created datasets are not particularly useful to us, since they represent only collections of anonymous transactions.  However, if each line of our file represented customers, and included a customer ID number or demographic information, these datasets could be very informative to our marketing department, as well as excellent prospects for further data mining with PolyAnalyst’s other algorithms.

The second part of the report contains product association rules:

 

The rules, shown at the left side of the chart, can be read as product associations.  The last rule in the above illustration, for instance, could be read as "If a customer buys Product 09C, they will probably also buy Product 01L".  Note that these rules are directional. For example, the rule that contains the same product codes, but in the opposite order is not present in the table.  This rule has not passed the test either for Minimum support, improvement, or confidence.

 

Not all the rules are created equal.  The three numbers listed after the rule show how reliable it is.  The Confidence percentage shows the percent chance, given a customer’s purchase of one group of items, that they will also purchase another item. In addition, two significance measures are provided – the Support indicates in what percentage of all transactions this combination occurs, while the Improvement indicates how much better the Confidence of this rule is than random chance.  In the last line of the above example, you can see that customers who buy Product 09C have an 84.87% chance of also buying Product 01L.  This is supported by 10.98% of the transactions, and is 3.5488 times more likely than random chance would indicate.

 

These numbers are calculated rather simply – of transactions that included Product 09C, 84.87% of them also included Product 01L, so the confidence is 84.87%. Out of all the transactions in the World dataset, 10.98% of them included both Product 09C and Product 01L, so support is 10.98%.  The improvement of 3.5488 tells us that Product 01L is included in 23.92% (= 84.87 / 3.5488) of all transactions in the World dataset – thus, knowing with 84.87% certainty that a customer will buy Product 01L is 3.5488 times better than random chance.  This is a good predictive rule.

Step 4: Further Analysis

It is possible that these results are exactly what you are looking for.  However, it is also possible that your initial exploration has returned too many or two few groups and rules.  In this case, it is necessary to run the exploration again, this time with a different minimum support percentage of 15.

1.      Right-click on the World dataset and choose Explore | Basket Analysis again.

2.      This time, choose a minimum support of 15%, leaving other parameters as before.

3.      Name the process BA_15_2_65 in the Process Name text box.

4.      Click OK to run the exploration.

In under a minute, PolyAnalyst responds with another report and two new datasets. This time, only three groups of products were found:

 

 

Note that these product groups were all found in the previous analysis as well; only they were grouped with more products into larger groups.  For example, with a minimum support of 10%, products 06K, 02C, 02D, 01D, 01C, and 01B were a group – with a minimum support of 15%, products 06K, 02D, and 01B no longer makes the cut. Product 01B has in fact moved to another group, being classified with 01I.

 

The Basket Analysis algorithm favors larger groups of products, but will not form groups with a minimum support less than the minimum you provide.  As a result, raising and lowering the minimum support can result in very different product groups.  All of these groups are valid – they are simply appropriate for different purposes.  Looking at the table of product association rules, we can see that the supports are much higher this time:

 

 

Since we increased the minimum support, the support for all the considered clusters, and correspondingly, the confidence of association rules has increased too.  All of these rules are of good quality – the support and confidences are all high enough to make it worthwhile for the marketing department to attempt cross selling, and the improvements are greater than 1. (An improvement less than 1 would indicate that the confidence shown is actually less than random chance, and the rule should not be considered for cross selling.)

 

Step 5: Finishing the Exploration

In our case, the result from the analysis ran with minimum support 10% was most appropriate.  The electronics manufacturer wanted several clusters of products that could be recommended by their marketing personnel, as well as five to ten association rules.  However, if your purposes required fewer or more groups of products or association rules, with higher or lower confidence and support, you could continue to fine-tune the three user-controlled parameters of the exploration (minimum support, confidence, and improvement) until obtaining the results you want.  One of the key advantages to PolyAnalyst’s basket analysis algorithm is that it works so quickly that reapplying it many times is feasible.   Try running the analysis again with the same minimum support of 15% and improvement 2, but lowering the required minimum confidence to 55% to see if this results in more product groups and association rules.

1.      Right-click on the World dataset and choose Explore | Basket Analysis again.

2.      This time, choose a minimum support of 15% like the last example.

3.      Choose a minimum confidence of 55% by typing in 55 into the minimum confidence text box.

4.      Name the process BA_15_2_55 in the Process Name text box.

 

5.      Click OK.

 

In fact, you will see that with these parameters Market Basket Analysis finds precisely the same groups of products as in the previous exploration, since the minimum support was selected to be the same!  At the same time, PolyAnalyst finds two new association rules for products in these groups, 01K -> 02B and 01B -> 01I, with a slightly lower confidence level (which might still be high enough to represent a promising cross-sell opportunity!)
